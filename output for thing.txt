Comprehensive Security Report – January 1‑5 2024
(All timestamps are UTC unless otherwise noted)

1. Executive Summary
The log set shows a high‑volume, multi‑vector attack campaign targeting authentication, web‑application endpoints, and internal services. The most critical findings are:

#	Critical Indicator	Why it matters
1	Remote‑Code‑Execution (RCE) attempts on /wp‑admin – multiple IPs (203.0.113.1, 192.168.1.100) tried to upload/execute back‑door payloads.	Successful RCE would give full control of the web server.
2	Directory‑Traversal to /../../etc/passwd – 10.0.0.150, 104.26.10.78, 203.0.113.1, 45.33.32.156.	Could expose system password files and other secrets.
3	Malicious file uploads (backdoor.jsp, shell.php) – 15 distinct events across 6 IPs.	Provides persistent foothold; many uploads succeeded (HTTP 200/201).
4	Credential‑stuffing / brute‑force login bursts – ≥ 12 distinct bursts from 185.220.101.1, 45.33.32.156, 203.0.113.1, 10.0.0.1, 192.168.10.44.	Indicates automated password‑spraying; several accounts (sysadmin, root, monitor) were targeted.
5	SQL‑Injection (SQLi) and Command‑Injection attempts – ≥ 14 events, many using sqlmap/1.6.	Successful exploitation could lead to data leakage or further code execution.
6	DDoS‑style traffic spikes – 185.220.101.1 and 45.33.32.156 generated repeated high‑frequency requests (e.g., 03:16:00, 07:50:48).	Could degrade service availability.
7	Outbound connections to known malicious hosts – 45.33.32.156, 203.0.113.1, 185.220.101.1 observed from internal IPs (10.0.0.150, 172.16.0.150, 10.10.10.5).	Possible data exfiltration or C2 channel.
If any of the above succeed, the impact ranges from full system compromise to credential theft, data loss, and service outage.

6. Conclusion
The logs reveal a coordinated, multi‑stage attack leveraging credential‑stuffing, brute‑force, SQLi, command‑injection, and malicious file uploads to gain footholds on the web server. The most urgent actions are to contain the back‑door uploads, block the external malicious IPs, and reset privileged credentials. Follow‑up forensic analysis is required to confirm whether any of the RCE or directory‑traversal attempts succeeded and to ensure that no persistent agents remain on the network.
